Background

One of the most important topics of school reform today is the issue of school structure. School structure is often used interchangeable with "grade-span configuration" and other terms that are all used to describe the same thing: the organization of grades within a school building. For example, having kindergarten through eighth grade (K-8) grouped together in one building is one form of elementary school structure. Another is to separate the early grades (K-4 or K-5) from the older grades (6-8), which are what we typically consider a "middle school." Different again is the idea of combining grades 7-12 into a structure called the "junior high school."

Researchers and educators have fiercely debated the merits of each of these structures throughout the years. At certain points in the last century, each of the three structures described above has had its turn as the "dominant" structure in the public education landscape. Most recently, the K-8 structure is widely believed to be the "best".
